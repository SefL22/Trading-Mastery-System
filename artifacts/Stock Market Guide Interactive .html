Foundation Concepts

By the end of this guide, you'll be comfortable understanding and applying key stock market terms, making smart investments, and navigating the world of brokerage accounts.

01 Stocks

What are Stocks?

A stock represents a small ownership share in a company. When you buy stock, you're buying a piece of that company. Stocks are traded on the stock market, where buyers and sellers exchange shares.

Types of Stocks

  • Common Stock: The most common type of stock, giving you voting rights and a share of dividends
  • Preferred Stock: A type of stock that gives no voting rights but often pays a higher dividend
Example:

Let's say you buy 1 share of Apple Inc. (AAPL). This means you own a tiny part of Apple. If Apple's business does well, the value of your stock may increase, and you might make a profit when you sell it.

Key Concepts:
  • Stock Price: The current price of one share of a company
  • Ticker Symbol: The short code used to identify a company's stock (e.g., AAPL is the ticker for Apple)

02 The Stock Market

What is the Stock Market?

The stock market is a place where stocks (and other securities like bonds and ETFs) are bought and sold. You can think of it like a giant marketplace where buyers and sellers meet to trade shares of companies.

The two main exchanges in the U.S. are:
  • New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) – The oldest and largest stock exchange in the world
  • NASDAQ – A technology-heavy exchange where companies like Apple, Amazon, and Google are listed

03 ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

What is an ETF?

An ETF is like a basket that holds a collection of stocks, bonds, or other assets. It allows you to invest in a broad range of companies without buying individual stocks.

Example:

SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) holds shares from the 500 largest companies in the U.S. (like Apple, Microsoft, and Amazon). By buying one share of the SPY ETF, you're investing in all 500 companies at once.

Why Use ETFs?

Diversification: By owning an ETF, you spread your risk across many companies, rather than putting all your money into one stock.

04 Dividends

What is a Dividend?

A dividend is a payment that some companies make to their shareholders, usually in the form of cash. It's like a reward for owning stock in the company.

Example:

Coca-Cola (KO) pays a quarterly dividend to its shareholders. If you own 100 shares of Coca-Cola, and the dividend is $0.50 per share, you would receive $50 in dividend payments each quarter.

Why Do Companies Pay Dividends?

Dividends are often paid by well-established companies with stable earnings. They reward investors and attract more people to buy the stock.

05 Yields

What is a Yield?

A yield is the percentage return on an investment. It's often used to describe how much income (like dividends) an investment generates relative to its price.

Example:

If Coca-Cola's stock price is $50 per share, and it pays a dividend of $2 per share annually, the dividend yield is 4% ($2 ÷ $50 = 0.04 or 4%).

Formula:

Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend ÷ Stock Price

06 Bull Market vs. Bear Market

What is a Bull Market?

A bull market refers to a period when the stock market (or a specific stock) is rising or expected to rise. Investors are confident and optimistic.

Example:

The stock market experienced a bull market in 2017, where many stocks, including Amazon and Tesla, were rising in value.

What is a Bear Market?

A bear market is the opposite — it's when the stock market or a specific stock is falling or expected to fall. Investor confidence is low.

Example:

The stock market entered a bear market in 2008 during the financial crisis, where many stocks lost a lot of value.

07 Capital Gain and Capital Loss

What is a Capital Gain?

A capital gain is the profit you make when you sell a stock for more than you paid for it.

Example:

If you buy 10 shares of Amazon at $100 each ($1,000 total) and sell them for $150 each ($1,500 total), you made a capital gain of $500.

What is a Capital Loss?

A capital loss is when you sell a stock for less than you paid for it.

Example:

If you bought 10 shares of Netflix for $200 each ($2,000 total) and sold them for $150 each ($1,500 total), you experienced a capital loss of $500.

08 Market Order vs. Limit Order

What is a Market Order?

A market order is when you buy or sell a stock immediately at the current market price.

Example:

If you place a market order to buy 10 shares of Tesla at $250 each, your order will execute at the best available price.

What is a Limit Order?

A limit order is when you set a specific price at which you want to buy or sell a stock. The order will only execute when the stock reaches that price.

Example:

If you set a limit order to buy Apple at $130 per share, your order will only be filled if Apple's price drops to $130 or lower.

09 Risk and Reward

What is Risk and Reward?

Risk and reward are two sides of investing. Risk is the chance of losing money, and reward is the potential for making money. Generally, the higher the potential reward, the higher the risk.

Examples:
  • Stocks like Tesla or Bitcoin have higher potential rewards but also higher risk because their prices can change quickly
  • Stable companies like Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) or Procter & Gamble (PG) are generally lower risk but may offer slower growth

10 P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)

What is a P/E Ratio?

The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to its earnings (profits). It helps investors decide whether a stock is expensive or cheap.

Example:
  • If a company's stock is $100 per share and its earnings are $5 per share, the P/E ratio is 20 ($100 ÷ $5)
  • A higher P/E ratio might mean the stock is expensive, while a lower P/E ratio could mean it's cheap relative to earnings

11 Index

What is an Index?

An index is a collection of stocks that represents a particular market or sector. It helps investors measure the overall performance of a specific part of the stock market.

Examples:
  • The S&P 500 Index is made up of 500 of the largest U.S. companies, like Apple, Microsoft, and Amazon. It is often used to track the overall health of the U.S. stock market
  • The Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) is another index that tracks 30 major U.S. companies, including companies like Coca-Cola, McDonald's, and IBM
Why is an Index Important?

An index allows investors to get a snapshot of the market's performance without needing to track every individual stock.

12 Volume

What is Volume?

Volume refers to the number of shares of a stock that are traded during a specific period (usually one day). High volume means a lot of shares are being bought and sold, while low volume means less trading activity.

Example:

If Tesla's stock traded 10 million shares in one day, its volume for that day would be 10 million. If it only traded 1 million shares, the volume would be lower.

Why Does Volume Matter?

High volume can indicate strong investor interest, while low volume might suggest that investors are unsure or not interested in the stock.

13 Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

What is Market Capitalization?

Market capitalization (market cap) is the total value of a company's outstanding shares of stock. It is calculated by multiplying the stock's price by the number of shares outstanding.

Example:

If Apple (AAPL) has 5 billion shares outstanding and the price of one share is $200, its market cap would be: 5 billion shares × $200 = $1 trillion

Why Does Market Cap Matter?

Companies with a large market cap (like Apple) are often more stable and less risky, while small-cap companies can have higher growth potential but may also be riskier.

14 Blue-Chip Stocks

What are Blue-Chip Stocks?

Blue-chip stocks are shares of well-established companies that are known for their reliability, stability, and ability to pay dividends. These companies often have a long history of strong performance.

Examples:

Companies like Coca-Cola (KO), Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), and Microsoft (MSFT) are considered blue-chip stocks because they are large, well-established, and financially stable.

Why Invest in Blue-Chip Stocks?

Blue-chip stocks are generally considered safer investments because they tend to weather market downturns better and provide steady returns.

15 Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP)

What is a DRIP?

A Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP) allows investors to automatically reinvest the dividends they receive from a stock back into additional shares of that same stock. This can help grow your investment over time without having to do anything manually.

Example:

If you own 10 shares of Coca-Cola (KO) and receive $0.50 per share as a dividend, instead of receiving $5 in cash, a DRIP would automatically buy more shares of Coca-Cola for you with that $5.

Why Use DRIP?

DRIPs allow investors to benefit from compound growth, where the reinvested dividends purchase more shares, potentially increasing future dividend income.

16 Short Selling (Shorting)

What is Short Selling?

Short selling is a way to make money when you believe the price of a stock will decrease. You borrow shares of a stock and sell them at the current price, hoping to buy them back at a lower price later, returning them to the lender and pocketing the difference.

Example:

Let's say you borrow 10 shares of Netflix (NFLX) at $400 each and sell them for $4,000. If Netflix's stock drops to $350 per share, you can buy those shares back for $3,500 and return them to the lender, making a profit of $500.

Why Is Short Selling Risky?

If the stock price goes up instead of down, you can lose money, and there's no limit to how much you could lose (the price could keep rising).

17 IPO (Initial Public Offering)

What is an IPO?

An IPO is the first time a company offers its stock to the public. It's a way for companies to raise money by selling shares of ownership to investors.

Example:

Uber Technologies (UBER) went public in 2019 with an IPO. Before the IPO, Uber was a private company, but after the IPO, anyone could buy shares of Uber on the stock market.

Why Do Companies Have IPOs?

IPOs allow companies to raise capital to fund growth, pay down debt, or make acquisitions. It also provides an opportunity for early investors to sell their shares.

18 Bonds

What is a Bond?

A bond is a type of loan that investors give to a company or government in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the principal amount at the bond's maturity.

Example:

When the U.S. government issues bonds, it is borrowing money from investors. If you buy a U.S. Treasury bond, you're lending the government money, and in return, they pay you interest over a set period of time, usually a few years.

Why Invest in Bonds?

Bonds are considered safer investments compared to stocks because they offer regular interest payments and are less volatile. However, they also offer lower potential returns.

19 Volatility

What is Volatility?

Volatility refers to how much and how quickly the price of a stock or other financial asset changes. High volatility means the stock price can swing wildly, while low volatility means the price is more stable.

Examples:
  • Tesla (TSLA) is known for high volatility because its stock price can change a lot in a short period of time, especially based on news and earnings reports
  • Procter & Gamble (PG) has lower volatility because it is a stable, established company that doesn't experience large price swings
Why Does Volatility Matter?

Volatility can create opportunities for large gains, but it also increases the risk of big losses. Investors need to decide if they're comfortable with the level of volatility of a stock before investing.

20 Market Correction

What is a Market Correction?

A market correction occurs when stock prices drop by 10% or more from their recent highs. This can happen for various reasons, such as economic downturns or negative news affecting certain sectors.

Example:

In 2020, the stock market experienced a correction due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Many stocks, including those of airlines and cruise companies, lost more than 10% of their value.

Why Do Market Corrections Happen?

Market corrections are a natural part of the market cycle and can sometimes present buying opportunities for investors who believe the market will recover.

21 Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is the interest on an investment that is calculated not only on the initial amount of money you invested, but also on any interest that has been added to that amount.

Example:

If you invest $1,000 in a savings account with an interest rate of 5% per year, you'll earn $50 in interest after the first year. In the second year, you earn 5% interest on $1,050 (the original $1,000 plus the $50 interest), which equals $52.50 in interest for the second year.

Why is Compound Interest Important?

Compound interest can help your investments grow over time, especially if you reinvest earnings and let them build on each other.

22 Sector and Industry

What is a Sector?

A sector is a broad category of companies that operate in the same area of the economy. Examples of sectors include technology, healthcare, finance, and consumer goods.

Examples:
  • The Technology sector includes companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Alphabet (Google)
  • The Healthcare sector includes companies like Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, and Merck

What is an Industry?

An industry is a more specific category within a sector. For example, the technology sector includes industries like software, hardware, and semiconductors.

Examples:
  • The Software industry includes companies like Microsoft, Salesforce, and Adobe
  • The Semiconductor industry includes companies like Intel and Nvidia

23 Bull Market vs. Bear Market (Extended)

What is a Bull Market?

A bull market refers to a period when the stock market is generally rising or expected to rise. Investors are confident, and prices are trending upward.

Example:

From 2009 to 2020, the stock market experienced a long bull market following the 2008 financial crisis, with major stocks like Apple, Amazon, and Microsoft seeing significant growth.

What is a Bear Market?

A bear market is the opposite of a bull market. It occurs when stock prices are falling, often by 20% or more from their recent highs. Investor sentiment is negative, and there's a general sense of pessimism.

Example:

The COVID-19 pandemic caused a bear market in early 2020, with stock prices of companies like Airlines and Cruise Lines dropping significantly as the economy slowed down.

24 Earnings Report

What is an Earnings Report?

An earnings report is a quarterly (or annual) statement published by a company detailing its financial performance. It includes data such as revenue, profit, and expenses.

Example:

Apple releases an earnings report every quarter, showing how much money it made (revenue), how much it spent, and how much profit it earned. If Apple's earnings report beats expectations, its stock price might go up.

Why Are Earnings Reports Important?

Earnings reports can affect stock prices. Strong earnings may cause a stock's price to rise, while disappointing earnings may cause the stock to fall.

25 Fundamental Analysis

What is Fundamental Analysis?

Fundamental analysis is a method of evaluating a company's financial health, including its revenue, profit, competition, and growth potential. It involves looking at the company's earnings, management, products, and overall industry.

Example:

Investors who use fundamental analysis might study Microsoft's earnings, revenue growth, competitive position, and industry trends before deciding whether to buy its stock.

Why is Fundamental Analysis Important?

It helps investors determine the intrinsic value of a stock, helping them decide whether it's overvalued or undervalued based on the company's fundamentals.

26 Technical Analysis

What is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis involves studying past stock price movements and trading volumes to forecast future price movements. It uses charts and patterns to predict where a stock's price might go.

Example:

Investors might use chart patterns (like the Head and Shoulders pattern or Moving Averages) to make predictions about the future price direction of Tesla's stock.

Why is Technical Analysis Important?

It's useful for short-term traders who want to make decisions based on price trends and patterns, rather than the underlying company's financial health.

27 Asset Allocation

What is Asset Allocation?

Asset allocation is the practice of dividing investments among different types of assets, such as stocks, bonds, and cash. The goal is to balance risk and reward based on an individual's goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon.

Example:

A young investor might allocate 80% of their money into stocks and 20% into bonds, while an older investor might allocate 60% into bonds and 40% into stocks to reduce risk.

Why is Asset Allocation Important?

Diversifying investments across different assets can reduce the overall risk of your portfolio. The mix of assets depends on your financial goals and risk tolerance.

28 Market Sentiment

What is Market Sentiment?

Market sentiment refers to the overall attitude or feeling of investors toward the stock market or a particular stock. It can be bullish (positive) or bearish (negative), and it affects how people buy and sell stocks.

Example:

Amazon (AMZN) might experience bullish sentiment if the company releases strong earnings, leading to more buying of the stock. Conversely, if a company faces a scandal or poor earnings, bearish sentiment might drive the stock price lower.

Why is Market Sentiment Important?

Understanding market sentiment can help you predict short-term stock price movements, especially when sentiment is driven by news, earnings reports, or broader economic trends.

29 Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

What is Dollar-Cost Averaging?

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals (e.g., monthly) regardless of the stock price. Over time, this strategy lowers the average cost per share.

Example:

If you invest $100 every month in Apple stock, whether the stock is at $150 or $200 per share, you'll buy more shares when the price is lower and fewer shares when the price is higher. This helps smooth out the effects of market volatility.

Why Use Dollar-Cost Averaging?

DCA reduces the impact of volatility, preventing you from trying to time the market perfectly. It's a great strategy for long-term investors.

30 Margin Trading

What is Margin Trading?

Margin trading is borrowing money from a brokerage to buy more stocks than you could with your own cash. The brokerage loans you the money, and you pay it back with interest.

Example:

If you want to buy $10,000 worth of stock but only have $5,000, you can borrow the other $5,000 from a broker. However, you must pay back the loan with interest.

Why is Margin Trading Risky?

If the stock price goes down, you can lose more than your initial investment and be required to repay the borrowed funds. This makes margin trading a risky strategy.

31 Stock Split

What is a Stock Split?

A stock split happens when a company issues more shares to its existing shareholders, increasing the total number of shares while reducing the stock price proportionally. It doesn't affect the total value of your holdings, but it makes the stock more affordable for new investors.

Example:

If Tesla announces a 2-for-1 stock split, each shareholder would receive 2 shares for every 1 share they currently own. If the stock was priced at $2,000 before the split, it would drop to $1,000 per share after the split.

Why Do Companies Do Stock Splits?

Companies may do stock splits to make their stock more affordable to individual investors, which can increase liquidity and attract more buyers.

32 Risk Tolerance

What is Risk Tolerance?

Risk tolerance refers to the amount of risk you're willing to take on with your investments. It's influenced by factors like your financial goals, investment time frame, and emotional comfort with market fluctuations.

Example:

A young investor saving for retirement might have a high risk tolerance and invest in growth stocks, while a retiree looking to preserve wealth might have a lower risk tolerance and invest in bonds.

Why is Risk Tolerance Important?

Knowing your risk tolerance helps you build a portfolio that fits your financial goals and psychological comfort with market ups and downs.

33 ETFs vs. Mutual Funds

What is the Difference Between ETFs and Mutual Funds?

Both ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) and mutual funds are investment vehicles that pool money from many investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other assets.

The main difference is how they're traded:

  • ETFs are bought and sold on stock exchanges like individual stocks, and their prices fluctuate throughout the day
  • Mutual Funds are priced once a day, after the market closes, and you can only buy or sell them at that price
Examples:
  • SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) tracks the S&P 500 Index, and you can buy and sell shares of it during market hours
  • A Vanguard mutual fund might track the same S&P 500 Index, but it's bought and sold at the end of the trading day
Why Choose One Over the Other?

ETFs generally offer lower fees and more flexibility, while mutual funds may be better for long-term investors who don't mind less frequent trading.
